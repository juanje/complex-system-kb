# Externality

**Domain:** Complex systems, economics, social systems

An externality is the effect that an action or decision has on others who did not directly participate in that action or decision. Externalities can be positive (benefiting non-participants) or negative (harming them). Together with feedback mechanisms, externalities generate the interdependencies that make complex systems sensitive to perturbations — because a change in one element propagates through externalities to affect the entire system in unpredictable ways.

## Usage context
- Externalities can connect agents that have no direct relationship. In an ecosystem model, more grass leads to more herbivores, which leads to more food for wolves — even though grass and wolves never interact directly. The wolves benefit from the grass through a chain of externalities.
- More interdependencies (created by both feedback loops and externalities) mean more unpredictability. The accumulation of these chains of effects becomes increasingly difficult to foresee, which is a core reason why complex systems resist prediction and control.
- Positive externalities include a gardener improving their garden and increasing neighboring house values, or one team's innovation benefiting other teams in the organization. Negative externalities include a factory producing toxic waste that harms the surrounding environment, or one department's aggressive sales creating delivery problems for operations.
- Externalities are a key mechanism in cascading effects: in the Schelling segregation model, each agent's decision to move creates externalities for all its neighbors, potentially pushing them past their own tolerance thresholds.
