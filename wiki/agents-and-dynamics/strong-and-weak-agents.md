# Strong and Weak Agents

Agents in complex systems exist on a spectrum from weak to strong. Weak agents follow fixed, simple rules without learning — their behavior is predictable because they react only based on known rules (atoms following physics laws, for instance). Strong agents, by contrast, are complex systems in their own right: they have memory, learning capacity, and the ability to create new rules on top of the basic ones. This makes their behavior far less predictable and the systems they compose far more dynamic.

## Key points
- **Weak agents** are predictable because their reactions follow known simple rules. An atom reacting to electromagnetic forces, a cell responding to pH changes — these agents produce complex system behavior through sheer numbers and interaction, not through individual sophistication.
- **Strong agents** incorporate memory, learning, internal perception, and meta-rules (rules about rules). Because these additional layers are internal and unknown to other agents, they dramatically increase the system's unpredictability.
- Strong agents are typically complex systems in their own right, which connects directly to the multilevel nature of complex systems: a person is an agent in a team, but is also a complex system made of organs, cells, and biochemical processes.
- More intelligent animals play more and in more varied ways, suggesting a deep link between agent strength (capacity for learning and meta-rule creation) and the emergence of exploratory, playful behavior.
- **Agency as inhibition.** A fundamental capacity of strong agents is not just acting but also NOT acting -- inhibiting the response the system naturally tends to produce. This is costly and is the first capacity to degrade under load (stress, fatigue, pressure).
- **Under pressure, agents revert to old attractors.** Because the inhibition capacity is exhausted first when the system is overloaded, behavior change fails under stress even when the agent "knows better" -- the old attractor fires before the new pattern can be selected.

## Meta-rules

Strong agents create **meta-rules** — higher-order rules that layer on top of the basic simple rules of the system. Meta-rules do not replace the original rules but add complexity to behavior. They are closely related to thresholds: crossing a threshold can trigger a change in which meta-rules apply, altering the agent's behavior pattern.

Because meta-rules are internal to the agent and unknown to others, they are a major source of unpredictability in systems composed of strong agents. A chess player who develops personal strategies, openings, and preferred moves through experience has created meta-rules that go beyond the game's official rules — and opponents cannot anticipate them.

## Examples
- **Chess player:** The game has fixed simple rules, but through experience a player develops openings, strategies, and tactical patterns (meta-rules) unknown to opponents. This makes strong agents progressively harder to predict as they accumulate experience.
- **Slime mold:** Individual cells that are weak agents individually, but collectively behave as a single organism capable of solving mazes, finding optimal city-connecting networks, and anticipating events — all through simple local rules like seeking food and secreting chemical signals.
- **Wolf cubs at play:** Young wolves learn to stalk, chase, flee, and bite through play with siblings. They develop meta-rules about when to bite hard versus softly, when to chase versus flee — calibrating their behavior through exploratory experience rather than fixed instinct.
- **Athlete learning a new technical gesture.** Under pressure, the system "wants" to revert to the old pattern trained for years; agency is demonstrated by braking the automatic response -- inhibiting the old attractor so the new pattern has space to emerge.
