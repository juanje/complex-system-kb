# Behavior as Emergent Property

Behavior does not originate "from within" (brain plans, decides, executes) or "from without" (environment molds, conditions, reinforces) but emerges from the continuous, dynamic relationship between an embodied individual and a multi-layered environment. Two historical traditions -- internalist and externalist -- each capture something real but share a fundamental error: treating individual and environment as separate entities. The third way recognizes that individual and environment co-constitute each other: you cannot define one without the other. This reframing has profound practical consequences: to change behavior, it is insufficient to give instructions, transmit information, or "decide" to change -- one must modify the conditions of the system so that it reorganizes in a new direction.

## Key points

- **Neither internalist nor externalist but co-constitutive.** The internalist view (brain generates behavior) and the externalist view (environment shapes behavior) both fail because they treat individual and environment as separate entities that interact. In reality, person and environment form a single system. A person who has sat 8 hours a day for years is not "a normal person using a chair" but a person-chair system that has produced specific postural attractors, ranges of motion, and perceptions of bodily capability. The chair is not background scenery -- it is part of the system producing the behavior.

### Three layers of environment

- **Physical external environment.** Surfaces, objects, light, sounds, obstacles -- what surrounds us and offers opportunities for action. This is the most visible layer, but only one of three.
- **Internal environment (the body itself).** Physiological state, fatigue, pain, hunger, emotional state, hormones. This layer deeply modulates how the external environment is perceived: stairs "offer" something very different when rested vs. exhausted. The body is not just the vehicle for action -- it is an environment that shapes what actions are possible and perceived.
- **Sociocultural environment.** Norms, values, expectations, language, tools, technology, relationships. This layer shapes goals, intentions, and interpretation of situations. Being told "stairs are dangerous for your back" tints that affordance with threat, regardless of whether the stairs actually pose a risk.
- **All three interact to produce behavior.** A single behavior cannot be explained by looking only "inside" or "outside" the person. The three layers modulate each other continuously, and behavior emerges from their interplay.

### Affordances and the perception-action cycle

- **We perceive affordances, not abstract properties.** Gibson: a step "offers" climbing to someone who can flex their knees and lift their feet, but not to someone in a wheelchair. Affordances are emergent properties of the organism-environment interaction -- they exist in the relationship, not in the object or the person alone.
- **Perception and action form a continuous loop.** We do not perceive then act as two sequential steps. We perceive to move and move to perceive. Each action changes the relationship with the environment and generates new information guiding the next action.
- **Affordances are modulated by all three environmental layers.** Fatigue (internal) changes what a flight of stairs "offers." Being told "stairs are dangerous for your back" (sociocultural) tints that affordance with threat. The physical structure of the stairs (external) provides the base, but what they "afford" depends on the full organism-environment system.
- **Extended cognition integrates into this loop.** Tools, notebooks, computers, and even other people can become part of the cognitive process, not mere external aids. The perception-action cycle extends beyond the body.

### Agency as inhibition

- **Agency is the capacity to NOT act.** When changing a trained pattern, the old attractor is ready to fire under pressure, fatigue, or stress. Agency in that moment is demonstrated not by executing the new pattern but by *braking the old one*. This is a fundamental and costly manifestation of being a strong agent.
- **Inhibition degrades first under load.** Inhibition requires resources, attention, and practice. It is the first capacity to degrade when the system is overloaded, which is why under pressure people revert to old patterns. To change a behavior, it is not enough to learn "the new" -- one must also develop the capacity to inhibit "the old," and that requires practice in conditions approaching real situations, not just ideal ones.

### Behavioral flexibility as intelligence

- **Herculano-Houzel: intelligence is repertoire, not computation.** Behavior in the broadest sense is any observable action driven by energy flow (even a moon orbiting). What the nervous system adds is not behavior itself but repertoire -- the ability to do different things in the same situation.
- **Flexibility vs. stereotypy defines the continuum.** A plant always grows toward light. A fan always spins the same way. But a dog sometimes chases the ball, sometimes ignores it, sometimes brings it back. This flexibility is what the nervous system provides, and it is what Herculano-Houzel identifies as intelligence.
- **This connects to exploration, diversity, and attractors.** Behavioral flexibility maps directly onto the exploration-exploitation trade-off, the role of diversity and variability, and the depth and distribution of attractors. All are mechanisms that modulate how flexibly a system behaves.
- **Intelligence is behavioral repertoire, not computational power.** Intelligence is the capacity to vary actions adaptively — behavioral repertoire enabled by the nervous system (Suzana Herculano-Houzel's framing). Even organisms without nervous systems exhibit behavior, but they lack the capacity to respond differently to the same situation.
- **Individual and environment co-constitute each other.** A person who has sat 8 hours a day for years is not "a normal person using a chair" but a person-chair system that has produced specific postural attractors, movement ranges, and self-perceptions. This rejects both internalism (brain generates behavior) and externalism (environment shapes behavior).
- **Three interacting environmental layers produce behavior.** Physical external (surfaces, objects, light), internal bodily (fatigue, pain, emotional state, hormones), and sociocultural (norms, values, medical advice). A single behavior emerges from all three layers interacting — interventions targeting only one layer are likely insufficient.
- **Embodied anticipation: simulating without executing.** The nervous system can simulate the consequences of actions without executing them. The brain activates the same motor and sensory circuits it would use for real execution, just partially. Expert intuition is explained by superior simulation capacity from internalized experience. Simulation quality is bounded by experience and attractor strength.

## Examples

- **Sedentarism as person-chair co-constitution.** The person-chair system has co-constituted specific postural attractors, movement ranges, and self-perception of physical capacity. Years of sitting have not merely placed a person in a chair but reorganized the entire system -- muscular, perceptual, attitudinal -- around that pattern. Changing "the behavior" requires changing the system, not just giving advice.
- **Chronic back pain and stairs.** The physical environment has stairs. The internal environment (pain and fear of movement) makes those stairs feel like a threat. The sociocultural environment (doctors' warnings, internet advice, family counsel saying "be careful with your back") reinforces that perception. Same stairs, three layers of environment, one emergent behavior: avoidance.
- **An athlete changing a trained gesture.** Under pressure, the system "wants" to revert to the old technical pattern that has been trained for years. Agency in that moment is freeing oneself by inhibiting that automatic response -- a costly act that degrades first when the athlete is fatigued, stressed, or overloaded.
- **A dog and a ball.** Sometimes the dog chases it, sometimes ignores it, sometimes brings it back, sometimes takes it to its corner -- the same stimulus, different responses, because the nervous system enables repertoire. A fan, by contrast, always spins the same way. The difference is behavioral flexibility.
- **A step and a wheelchair user.** A step affords climbing to someone with functional legs but not to someone in a wheelchair. The affordance exists in the relationship between organism and environment, not in the object or the person alone. Change the body (rehabilitation), change the environment (ramp), or change the sociocultural context (accessibility norms) and the affordance transforms.
- **Jumping over a puddle.** Before you jump, your body "calculates" whether it can make it — not through physics equations but by simulating the jump based on what your body has done before. The decision often occurs before conscious awareness.
