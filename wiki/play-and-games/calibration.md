# Calibration

Calibration is the process by which an agent, through repeated practice in representative environments, refines the connection between perception and appropriate response. It is the tuning of the perception-action loop -- achieving increasingly efficient and effective reactions by closing the gap between what an agent perceives and how it responds. Calibration is not the same as technique drilling: it requires context, other agents, and the dynamics of real (or realistically simulated) systems.

## Key points

- **Calibration requires context.** Practicing skills in isolation -- without other agents and outside the system -- misses the critical connection between perception and action. Real systems are dynamic, so agents must learn to adapt responses to changing conditions, not just execute techniques in a vacuum. A punch practiced on air teaches the motion; a punch practiced in sparring teaches when, where, and how hard to throw it.
- **First exposure to unknown situations produces poor responses.** The first time an agent faces an unknown situation, especially under stress, it will respond poorly. This is expected, not a failure. Play and simulation provide safe environments to fail, adjust, and refine responses iteratively before facing real consequences.
- **Realism improves transfer.** More realistic simulations (within safety limits) produce better calibration. The gap between training and reality determines how well responses transfer. This is why firefighter drills that closely simulate actual fire conditions, and martial arts sparring that closely simulates real combat, produce agents better prepared for the real thing.
- **Calibration is ongoing, not one-time.** Because complex systems are dynamic and conditions change, calibration is a continuous process. An agent calibrated for one set of conditions may need to recalibrate when the system evolves -- new opponents, new environments, new constraints.

## Examples

- **Puppies learning bite force.** Puppies that play-bite learn their jaw strength, pain thresholds, and the difference between play and aggression. The feedback from siblings -- yelping, withdrawing, retaliating -- provides the perceptual information the puppy needs to calibrate its responses. Dogs that never play as puppies become more aggressive adults because they never calibrated their bite force or learned to read situations proportionally.
- **Firefighter drills.** Firefighters train in environments that simulate real fire conditions -- heat, smoke, reduced visibility, time pressure. The closer the drill approximates reality, the better the calibration of their perception-action responses when they face an actual fire. Drills that only cover theory or use sterile environments produce weaker calibration.
- **Martial arts sparring.** Sparring is a controlled game where fighting is practiced within the reduced system of the martial art's rules. It allows agents to calibrate combat responses -- timing, distance, force, reading the opponent -- in a way that technique drills alone cannot. MMA competitions have shown that fighters who spar regularly perform better than those who only drill techniques, because sparring calibrates perception and action together.
- **Strength training adaptation.** A strength exercise does not make you stronger today -- there is a lag between the perturbation and the adaptation. Calibration through repeated training sessions allows the body to self-organize its response to progressively challenging loads.
