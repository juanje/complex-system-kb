# Skill Development

Developing skills to handle complex problems requires repeated exposure to the problem in varied contexts, embracing errors as essential feedback, and building perception-action calibration rather than memorizing techniques. In complex systems, learning is functional: the system learns what will be useful in the future, and the clearer the usefulness (high frequency or survival-critical), the deeper and more lasting the learning. Learning stems from the system's self-organization around a situation; it cannot be directed from outside, is neither predictable nor linear, and always starts from error-laden solutions that get refined through exploration.

## Key points

- **Traditional method problems:** The traditional approach -- learn theory, memorize techniques, repeat solutions, avoid errors -- presupposes that techniques exist for complex problems, limits you to known solutions, focuses on applying solutions rather than reading the problem, and actively minimizes errors that are fundamental to learning.
- **Alternative method principles:** Expose yourself to the problem to understand what needs solving. Repeat solving the problem, not the solution. Errors are expected, normal, and necessary -- "without information about whether you are doing well or poorly, exploration has no meaning." Scale the problem down rather than decomposing it into parts.
- **Characteristics of skills for complex problems:** Skills must be flexible (the problem is dynamic), adaptive (learning and evolving over time), balanced between exploration and exploitation, and sensitive to the environment. Environmental sensitivity is "perhaps the most important characteristic" -- if you cannot perceive changes, you cannot adapt and have nothing to learn from.
- **Problem scaling vs. decomposition:** Instead of decomposing a complex problem into isolated parts (which destroys the complexity that makes it a complex problem), scale it down to a smaller but still representative version. Scaling techniques include grouping elements, eliminating non-essential elements, fixing certain variables, and working on sub-problems that are complex in themselves. The scaled version must maintain multiple information sources, require decision-making, and preserve context-dependency.
- **Path dependence:** Prior decisions and events constrain future possibilities in an irreversible way. For the same current situation, previous decisions determine available future possibilities. This is why recipes or playbooks from past successes cannot be reliably replicated -- the conditions, decisions, and path are different each time.
- Games, simulations, and reduced versions allow safe, frequent exposure to aspects of the problem that rarely occur in reality, and random events can be injected that have never been encountered before.

## Examples

- Organizing a conference: even after a successful event, you cannot reproduce what you did and get the same success. If you analyze what to improve, you could keep refining endlessly -- it is a problem without a final solution.
- Software applications: no application exists without continuous updates and version changes, because an application is a static solution to a dynamic real-world problem.
- For a congress: practice with the same congress changing location, number of attendees, speakers, duration (same problem with different variables) or practice with different types of congresses with different themes and audiences (similar but different problems). Experience the problem from different perspectives: as organizer, attendee, speaker, volunteer.
- Catering for a congress: initially you must explore extensively to find the best option; once you have a selection, you can exploit those choices next time; but periodically you must explore the market again in case needs or options have changed.
- Conference organizing with scaling: group elements (one person represents a team), fix variables (budget, number of speakers), work on sub-problems separately while maintaining representativeness, and design games or simulations emphasizing areas with least experience and adding random events.
