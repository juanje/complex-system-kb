# Direction Over Destination

When facing complex problems, the most effective strategy is to orient around a direction of travel rather than a fixed endpoint. Because complex problems are dynamic -- they change with every intervention, every passage of time, and every action by other agents -- the "destination" may move, disappear, or transform while you work toward it. Clinging to a specific goal makes you rigid and unable to adapt; maintaining a direction provides coherent purpose while allowing continuous adjustment to the system's evolving state. As the author puts it: "you don't know the solution in advance; you have a direction and a final objective, but there are a thousand paths to get there, and the objective itself may move."

## Key points

- A fixed goal assumes the problem is static -- that the "destination" will still exist when you arrive. In complex systems, this assumption breaks down because the problem adapts to your interventions and to forces beyond your control. Direction preserves intentionality without demanding that the future match your current plan.
- Direction-oriented navigation naturally produces iterative, adaptive behavior: observe the system's current state, intervene toward your direction, observe again, adjust. This directly counteracts "turn-based thinking" -- the error of treating the system as static between your actions.
- The principle applies across domains. In health: the author abandoned fixed medium-long-term training plans for clients with chronic pain, instead setting a direction and adjusting each session to the person's current state. In business: a tech startup with a one-year plan ignores that the market will change; a direction allows continuous adaptation. In daily life: having a driving destination is not the same as having a fixed route.
- Direction over destination does not mean having no goals. It means holding goals loosely -- as orientations that inform each next step rather than as contracts that must be fulfilled regardless of changed circumstances.

## Examples

- Training clients with chronic pain: rather than committing to a fixed 12-week program, the author sets a direction (improve mobility, reduce pain sensitivity) and adjusts each session based on how the client presents that day -- because their complex system has changed since last session.
- Starting a tech company: a one-year business plan becomes obsolete because by launch time, the identified market need may have changed, new competitors may have appeared, or technology may have shifted. A direction ("solve coordination problems for remote teams") survives these shifts.
- Driving across a city: the destination is fixed, but clinging to one specific route when streets are closed, traffic is unexpected, or an emergency arises makes you less flexible and efficient. Navigation apps continuously recalculate -- they maintain direction while adapting the path.
