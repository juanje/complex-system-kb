# Complex Problems

Complex problems are dynamic, adaptive problems that change in response to interventions and internal system dynamics. Unlike complicated problems, which remain fixed once understood and can be solved with enough expertise, complex problems mutate as you interact with them. Any intervention alters the problem itself in unpredictable ways, which means complex problems cannot be fully solved -- only managed one version at a time. Top-down control does not work; instead, one must influence the system by adjusting parameters like diversity, interconnection, incentives, and environment so that self-organization moves in a desired direction.

## Key points

- The clearest differentiator is dynamism: complex problems change and adapt to what happens inside and outside the system, because the system self-organizes through agent interactions, feedback mechanisms, and attractors. A bridge stays built; a child never stops developing.
- When qualified experts have significant disagreements about the basic concepts of how to solve a problem, the problem is most likely complex (Todd Hargrove). This heuristic provides a practical test: if domain experts cannot agree on fundamentals, you are dealing with complexity rather than mere difficulty.
- An actor in a complex system "controls almost nothing, but influences almost everything" (Scott E. Page). This reframing -- from control to influence -- is the foundational shift required when approaching complex problems.
- Hard (difficult) problems are fixed problems with many possible solutions that depend on our decisions (e.g., the travelling salesman problem). Complex problems are dynamic problems that change based on the actions of others, not just our own decisions. The distinction matters because optimization is possible and desirable for hard problems but counterproductive for complex ones.

## Strategies to avoid

- **Divide and conquer:** In complex systems the relationships and interactions between elements are as important as the elements themselves. Unlike a Swiss watch or a car, you cannot disassemble a complex system into parts and understand it.
- **Reductionist/simplistic solutions:** Ignoring the system's interconnections, interdependencies, diversity, and feedback mechanisms produces an accurate but incomplete picture -- like the parable of the blind men and the elephant, where each person describes a different part accurately but none sees the whole.
- **Complicated solutions:** Attempting to account for all possible cases fails because many variables in complex systems are unknown, emergent, or constantly shifting. The result is a static, complicated solution that becomes a new problem.
- **Complex solutions:** A complicated solution that grows exponentially in interactions eventually creates a new complex problem layered on top of the original one -- the cure becomes a disease of its own.

## Multidisciplinary teams

Complex problems are studied by very different disciplines with very different viewpoints. No single perspective captures the full reality. Generalists who understand multiple areas without being experts in each are especially valuable: they see the global picture and establish connections that specialists miss, bridging communication gaps between fields. More diversity in knowledge and ways of thinking leads to better exploration of possible solutions and more innovation.

## Dynamic solutions

Some systems can develop flexible, dynamic skills that solve the various mutations of a problem, functioning as an adaptive subsystem within the larger system. The key is developing the ability to adapt solutions to the system's characteristics at each moment, not applying pre-established techniques. Agent-based models can serve as dynamic tools for exploring different interventions through simulation, but the real power lies in building adaptive capacity directly.

## Examples

- Educating a child, a person's health, organizational culture, a pandemic, a natural disaster, a political crisis, or preventing a stampede at a concert -- all are complex problems where any intervention changes the problem itself.
- Building a bridge is a fixed, known problem solvable with a plan. But you cannot make people follow your plan during a crisis, because each person acts based on countless factors they themselves do not fully know or control.
- The immune system constantly uses exploration to detect threats and switches to exploitation when it identifies one, adapting to whatever attacks the organism encounters -- a paradigm of dynamic solutions.
- The locomotor system constantly self-organizes to produce optimal solutions to coordinating many interconnected mobile parts efficiently (the degrees of freedom problem in motor control).
- The travelling salesman problem: 5 cities yield 12 possible routes, 10 cities yield 181,440, 30 cities yield over 4x10^30 -- exponentially hard but fixed. Phileas Fogg's trip, by contrast, depends on other actors (delays, customs, strikes, theft), making it complex rather than merely hard.
- The parable of the blind men and the elephant: each person touching a different part gives an accurate but incomplete description. All are right from their perspective, but none sees the complete reality.
