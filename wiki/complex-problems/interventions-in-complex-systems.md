# Interventions in Complex Systems

Interventions are designed actions that modify system parameters -- objectives, focus, limits, constraints -- to influence system behavior. No intervention is definitive. Because the system is dynamic and adaptive, interventions must constantly evolve with the system. Pre-designing all interventions from the start is impossible because each intervention changes the system state, and interventions interact with other forces acting on the system in ways that cannot be fully predicted. This demands humility about what we know relative to what the system knows.

## Key points

- Interventions must distinguish between temporary changes (immediate self-organization) and long-term changes (adaptation, learning). Imposing a chain formation and specific roles on a water-carrying team changes behavior immediately but may not produce lasting learning. The difference between a temporary response and genuine adaptation is critical for evaluating intervention success.
- **The performance-learning paradox:** Good performance now does not imply learning has occurred, and poor performance now does not mean no learning has happened. Immediate results of an intervention can be misleading; because adaptation takes time, short-term and long-term effects can be very different or even opposite. A strength exercise does not make you stronger today, but the adaptation will show next time.
- Each intervention changes the system state, which means the next intervention must be designed for the new state, not the original one. This makes intervention design inherently iterative: observe, intervene, observe again, adjust. The system provides continuous feedback that must be monitored.
- Interventions interact with other forces acting on the system. Because it is impossible to know all other interactions or predict their combined effect with our intervention, the principle is: intervene small, observe widely, adjust continuously. The belief that we know more than the system is the most dangerous assumption an intervener can hold.

## Examples

- **China's Four Pests Campaign (mid-20th century):** An intervention intended to eliminate sparrows -- classified as pests that ate grain -- destroyed the ecosystem instead of saving it. Without sparrows to eat them, locust populations exploded, causing a plague far more devastating than the sparrows ever were. This is the canonical example of believing you can change a complex system directly and predictably.
- Water-carrying team: imposing a chain formation and specific roles (one person draws water, one pours, others pass buckets) changes behavior immediately, but this externally imposed procedure may not produce lasting learning. The team has not self-organized; it has been constrained.
- Strength training: doing a strength exercise today does not make you stronger today. The adaptation occurs internally and shows up in future performance. Judging the intervention by immediate results would lead to the wrong conclusion.
- Prescribing an anti-inflammatory for a tendon without considering the broader system -- movement patterns, joint conditions, fitness level, usage intensity, stress, rest -- treats a symptom while the underlying complex problem continues to evolve.
