# System Influence Parameters

Complex systems cannot be controlled but can be influenced. They self-organize around objectives, guided by focus of attention, and adapt to limits and constraints. Modifying these four parameters -- objectives, focus of attention, limits, and constraints -- changes how the system behaves. Before designing any intervention, three questions must be answered: why do we want to intervene and what result do we expect; at what level (system or agents); and are we focused on the objective rather than prescribing the method. Interventions must address the "what" (objective), not the "how"; the system must self-organize to find the "how," because externally imposed procedures cannot account for the system's internal complexity and dynamics.

## Key points

- **Objectives (system vs. agent level):** Every complex system self-organizes around objectives, which can be defined at the system level (collective goal) or agent level (individual goals), with dramatically different effects. System-level objectives promote coordination and collective self-organization; agent-level objectives can fragment the system because each agent optimizes for its own goal with no incentive for cooperation. Systems can have primary objectives (survival) and temporary sub-objectives (finding food, fleeing danger) that shift dynamically.
- **Focus of attention:** Where the system or agents direct their information-seeking to achieve objectives. Changing the focus without changing the objective can produce different system behaviors, because self-organization depends on what information agents prioritize. Four mechanisms shift focus: incentives (what is valued or punished), limits and constraints (available resources and rules), information (what is shown or hidden and how it is presented), and capability building (making desired behaviors easier). The principle: "make easy what you want done more, make hard what you want done less."
- **Limits:** Define the shape, space, and resource boundaries within which a system can organize. Limits encompass space, time, available resources, and access between components. Changing any of these forces the system to reorganize. Parkinson's Law -- "work expands to fill the time available for its completion" -- is a daily example. This is not merely a conscious decision but a systemic response involving psychological, hormonal, and physiological factors; the entire organism self-organizes differently depending on the time limit. Counter-intuitively, tighter time limits increase the probability of task completion.
- **Constraints:** Rules governing what the system can do and how, operating at three levels: internal (what the system can do), external (rules imposed by environment), and task-specific (rules applying to the current action). Each level independently shapes behavior.
- **Degeneracy:** The capacity of different components or structures within a system to perform the same function. This is not redundancy (identical backups) but structurally different elements capable of performing equivalent functions. Degeneracy is a mechanism systems use to self-organize and adapt when objectives, limits, or constraints are modified -- multiple components can serve the same function, maintaining performance even when specific parts are unavailable.
- **Constraining (constriction):** A specific use of limits and constraints that forces a system in one particular direction, eliminating options so the system can only self-organize in one way. While there may be cases where constraining is appropriate, in general it contradicts the principle of allowing systems to self-organize and should be avoided.

## Examples

- Water-carrying team: giving the team a collective goal (carry the most water as a group) produces self-organized coordination (forming a chain), while giving each person an individual goal (carry the most water personally) produces uncoordinated individual effort. Same agents, same resources, different objective level, radically different behavior.
- Shoe company: the enterprise objective is "sell shoes," but each department (design, manufacturing, assembly, packaging, distribution, sales, marketing, customer service) has its own sub-objective contributing to the whole.
- Heel raise exercise: same objective (raise and lower heels while squeezing a tennis ball between ankles), but different foci of attention (reach highest point vs. squeeze the ball vs. maintain balance) produce different self-organization of the body.
- Anteater: when hungry, focuses attention on the ground seeking anthills; when a predator appears, attention shifts to finding trees or shelter. Same system, different focus, different behavior.
- A restaurant: limits include staff, space, location, and capital; constraints include menu, schedule, and food legislation. The menu itself is further constrained by regional food customs, target audience, ingredient prices, kitchen staff knowledge, and ingredient availability.
- Locomotion gait transitions: as speed increases, the body autonomously transitions from walking to trotting to running because each gait becomes more efficient at different speeds -- unconscious self-organization in response to changing limits.
- The author's "backpack law": regardless of backpack size, it always gets filled; therefore using a smaller backpack is the best way to carry fewer unnecessary items. A limit shapes self-organization.
- Neuronal plasticity: neurons or entire brain regions can take over functions of damaged areas -- degeneracy in action.
- Muscular compensation: muscles can perform the functions of other muscles when those are fatigued, atrophied, or injured.
- Well-known productivity methods as interventions: WIP (limits concurrent tasks), GTD (limits to one task and one context at a time), KISS (constrains against adding complexity), Pomodoro (25-minute time blocks), stand-up meetings (standing constraint focuses on brevity).
