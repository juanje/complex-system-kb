# Technique vs. Skill

A technique is a specific solution to a problem -- a set of methods, steps, or procedures -- while a skill is the capacity to create or find solutions for a type of problem, adapted to the current context. A technique is like a screwdriver: a fixed tool for a fixed problem. Your problem-solving capacity is limited by the number of techniques you know and your ability to choose the right one. A skill is like the ability to design your own tool based on the problem's particularities. For known, deterministic problems with few possible solutions (changing a tire, building a bridge, solving a quadratic equation), learning proven techniques is more cost-effective. For open, non-deterministic, dynamic, complex problems, only skills suffice.

## Key points

- Developing a skill takes more time and effort than learning a set of steps, which is why people default to learning techniques even when they know it is not the best option for complex problems. The path of least resistance leads to the wrong tool.
- **Context-sensitive problem-solving:** For complex problems, effective action requires reading and adapting to the current context rather than applying pre-set solutions. Techniques that worked for someone in specific circumstances probably will not produce the same result in other circumstances, or even for the same person a second time. You do not know the solution in advance; you have a direction and a final objective, but there are a thousand paths and the objective itself may move.
- **Skills as dynamic and evolving:** The author distinguishes "developing" skills from "acquiring" them. "Acquiring" implies something static that once obtained can be applied in any context. "Developing" implies ongoing change and evolution. Techniques are expressions of skill; a skill that has not changed over time is not a good or useful skill.
- **Difficulty vs. complexity:** The difficulty of a problem has nothing to do with its complexity. Designing a bridge is very difficult but not complex: the techniques are known and work the same way every time. Walking on a crowded sidewalk seems easy but is genuinely complex due to the interdependencies between moving people. This distinction determines whether techniques (for difficult problems) or skills (for complex problems) are the appropriate response.
- Skills are context-dependent: a skill developed in one context works best there, but as the context changes, the skill must evolve too. Environmental sensitivity is perhaps the most important characteristic -- if you cannot perceive your environment and its changes, you cannot adapt.

## Examples

- There are techniques for the Rubik's cube, Ikea furniture, bridges, tiramisu, and the travelling salesman problem -- all fixed, predictable problems where the same solution works every time.
- Walking on a crowded sidewalk without bumping into anyone, scheduling a meeting across time zones, and seating wedding guests are "simple-looking" but complex problems where techniques fall short.
- A crying baby may be crying for many different reasons; applying a trick from a video is like playing the lottery -- what matters is figuring out why the baby is crying and acting accordingly, differently each time.
- Back pain exercises that work for some people cause more pain in others; the same person in different circumstances presents different symptoms -- "the important thing is adapting to the person and the situation."
- Driving across a city: you may have the "perfect route" but a closed street, unexpected traffic, or a family emergency changes the problem -- clinging to one route makes you less flexible and efficient.
- The author's experience giving talks, conferences, seminars, workshops, classes, and interviews to audiences of all types -- if you cannot read the audience and adapt in the moment, you lose them within five minutes regardless of oratory techniques. The skill of public speaking had to evolve continuously across radically different contexts.
