# Black Swan Events

Black swan events are disproportionate, unexpected events that result from the non-linear properties of the complex system in which they occur. The concept was developed by [Nassim Taleb](../authors/nassim-taleb.md), who focused specifically on events that are most unforeseen (those that have never occurred before) and that create the greatest impact. Because they are products of non-linear dynamics within complex systems like the economy, climate, and sociopolitical contexts, they are inherent to complexity itself and cannot be reliably forecast despite some people's claims.

## Key points
- Natural catastrophes, wars, economic crises, and pandemics are events totally disproportionate to everyday occurrences in their contexts; because they are unpredictable products of non-linear dynamics, they cannot be reliably predicted. Anyone claiming to have predicted a black swan is engaging in hindsight bias -- after the fact, causes seem obvious, but before the event, the specific combination of factors was invisible.
- Large-scale events can be positive as well as negative; because non-linearity works in both directions, breakthroughs can be as disproportionate as catastrophes. The invention of the wheel, writing, and the Internet created immense civilizational changes compared to countless other technologies that had virtually no impact.
- [Nassim Taleb](../authors/nassim-taleb.md) contextualizes black swan events as products of non-linear properties within complex systems; his framework also distinguishes resilience (returning to a stable state after perturbation) from antifragility (improving because of perturbation), which is relevant to how systems can prepare for these extreme events. A system with multiple attractors is resilient but not necessarily antifragile.
- The core lesson of black swan theory for practitioners is not to try to predict specific extreme events -- which is impossible by definition -- but to build systems that can absorb and adapt to them when they inevitably occur. This connects directly to the concepts of diversity (more diverse systems are less fragile) and variability (systems with healthy variability can respond to novel perturbations).

## Examples
- The invention of the wheel, writing, and the Internet created immense civilizational changes compared to other technologies with virtually no impact. The discovery of penicillin had a completely unpredictable global health impact from what was an accidental discovery. These are positive black swans -- events that were as unforeseeable as catastrophes but with transformatively beneficial consequences.
- Virality: someone publishes videos with modest impact, then overnight goes viral with no apparent reason, no planning -- it simply happens. This is a non-linear, disproportionate event that no one can reliably engineer or predict. The system of social media distribution, with its millions of interconnected agents making share/ignore decisions, produces emergent patterns that cannot be reduced to any formula.
- The assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand did not cause World War I on its own, but in a highly interconnected system of alliances and tensions, this small event triggered a chain reaction that escalated into a global war -- illustrating how a perturbation in an interconnected, relatively stable system can produce a disproportionate response. The system was sitting near a tipping point, and a seemingly minor event pushed it over.
