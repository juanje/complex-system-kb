# Interconnection and Interdependence

The elements composing a complex system are connected to each other, and the behavior of some affects the others. This is likely the main reason that emergent properties arise in complex systems -- properties that do not exist in any individual component. The global function of a complex system is determined not by its individual parts but by the structure of connections and mutual interactions between them, producing capabilities no individual element could achieve alone.

## Key points
- Interconnection and interdependence are likely the primary reasons that new properties emerge in complex systems; because the interaction between two or more elements often gives rise to new properties or behaviors, complexity is an irreducibly relational phenomenon. The most relevant aspect of any complex system is how the structure of connections determines its global function.
- Connections can be physical (roads, neural pathways, bodily tissues) or abstract (commercial, social, diplomatic); therefore the concept applies across very different domains. Network science provides frameworks for characterizing these relationships along multiple dimensions: strong-weak, temporary-permanent, direct-indirect, symmetric-asymmetric, global-local, positive-negative, weighted-unweighted.
- The tight relationship between what happens to one individual or group and how it affects others is the interdependence that makes complex systems unpredictable; because a change in one element cascades through connections to affect the entire system, the most relevant aspect is the structure of connections, not the individual components.
- Complexity increases over time as connections multiply; because new relationships (friendships, rivalries, alliances, misunderstandings, reconciliations) form between elements, the web of interactions grows richer and less predictable. Externalities -- the effects that actions have on agents who did not participate in those actions -- connect agents that have no direct relationship, further increasing interdependency.
- Interconnections and interdependencies are one of the three principal sources of non-linearity in complex systems; because each agent's action is a reaction to its environment and other agents, and greater diversity among agents produces more varied responses, the system's behavior becomes increasingly unpredictable.

## Complexity from interactions

The global function of a complex system could not be carried out by any individual element on its own. As connections accumulate over time, complexity deepens. The author illustrates this with a "murder mystery" analogy: a murder on the first day of class (where nobody knows each other) produces few connections and simple investigation. A murder in a family business produces relationships of all kinds (love, hatred, jealousy, alliances, betrayals, inheritance expectations), making the map of connections far more complex. If the class stays together for years, it too would develop this increasing complexity through accumulated human interactions.

## Examples
- Neurons connect to form neural networks, which form functional brain regions, which connect to organs via nerves. Each organ sends sensory information back to the brain -- demonstrating constant bidirectional communication and mutual influence across the entire system.
- The nervous system connects with the endocrine system: detecting danger triggers physiological changes (sweating, increased heart rate). Conversely, increases in adrenaline and cortisol cause attention to narrow ("tunnel vision"), while elevated melatonin causes relaxation and sleepiness.
- In an ecosystem, an increase or decrease in one species' population can affect the entire ecosystem through chain effects -- a plague reduces food for one animal, which is food for another, creating cascading consequences.
- A road accident on a highway generates a traffic jam that can affect other roads and streets throughout the transportation network.
- John Snow (precursor of modern epidemiology) discovered the connection between cholera victims in 1854 London by mapping their interconnections. He found that all affected people shared the same contaminated water source. Creating a map and finding the interconnections was key to ending the outbreak, discovering the origin, and preventing future ones -- demonstrating the practical power of network thinking before the field was formalized.
