# Multilevel Organization

Complex systems are composed of elements that are themselves complex systems, creating a hierarchy of nested systems where each level has distinct properties and dynamics. This nesting means one cannot understand the whole by studying only one level -- what a cell does cannot predict the behavior of the organism it belongs to, and the dynamics between cities are not the same as between countries or between families.

## Key points
- Components of complex systems can also be complex systems, which gives rise to a hierarchy of nested levels; because of this nesting, reductionist analysis is insufficient. Behavior at one level cannot be reduced to behavior at another -- studying molecules cannot explain cellular behavior, studying cells cannot explain organ function, and studying organs cannot explain organism behavior.
- Each level has its own emergent properties and behaviors that are not evident when observing only the individual components; therefore, analyzing cellular functions cannot predict the liver's aggregate functions, let alone the organism's behavior. New structures, behaviors, and capabilities arise at each level.
- The multilevel structure extends both "downward" (from organism to systems, organs, tissues, cells, organelles, molecules) and "upward" (from individual to family, tribe, communities, countries, international organizations); because the dynamics differ at each level, each requires its own analysis while acknowledging its connections to other levels.
- The nesting creates a bidirectional hierarchy: looking "down" reveals ever smaller subsystems, and looking "up" reveals ever larger supersystems; because of this, any entity simultaneously belongs to multiple levels of organization, each with different rules and dynamics. A strong agent in a complex system is itself a complex system with internal agents, and what the agent does externally creates internal perturbations at lower levels.

## Nested systems

Systems contained within larger systems each maintain their own complexity while contributing to the dynamics of the larger whole. A human being is simultaneously an organism (containing systems, organs, cells), a family member, a community member, and a citizen -- each level with different dynamics and relationships. Because each nested level operates with its own rules, understanding any single level in isolation provides an incomplete and potentially misleading picture.

The nesting has practical implications: emergence creates new structures and behaviors at each level of nested systems (cells to organs to organ systems to organism) that cannot be deduced from lower-level rules. This adds unpredictability at each level and is one of the three principal sources of non-linearity in complex systems.

## Examples
- The Earth is a complex system containing living organisms, tectonic plates, and atmospheric phenomena. The Earth is part of the Solar System, which is part of the Milky Way galaxy -- each level a complex system nested within another, each operating with distinct dynamics.
- A liver cell carries out basic functions without awareness of how its work combines with other liver cells and systems (circulatory, nervous, endocrine). The liver's aggregate functions are very different from those of each individual cell and could not be deduced from cellular functions alone.
- The human body has levels going downward (human, systems, organs, tissues, cells, organelles, molecules) and upward (human, immediate family, extended group/tribe, communities, countries, groups of countries like the EU or WHO). At each level, distinct properties and relationships emerge.
- A baby exploring movement illustrates multi-level nesting: random head movements develop neck muscles (one level), visual tracking develops eye coordination (another level), and the combination of vestibular and visual input develops balance (yet another level). All happen through the baby's exploratory play, with external actions creating internal adaptations across multiple nested levels simultaneously.
