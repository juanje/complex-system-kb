# Resilience

Resilience is the capacity of a complex system to absorb perturbations and maintain or recover its functional organization. It is not rigidity — a rigid system resists perturbation until it breaks catastrophically. Resilience is the ability to bend without breaking, to absorb shock through multiple response options rather than a single deep defense. The wiki's fragile-volatile spectrum makes this concrete: fragile systems have few deep attractors and low diversity, so they are stable until a perturbation overwhelms them, at which point they shatter. Resilient systems maintain enough diversity, variability, and slack to absorb perturbations across a wide range of conditions.

## Key properties

- **Resilience requires diversity.** A system with diverse components, thresholds, and strategies can respond to a wider range of perturbations. When one response fails, others are available. Monocultures — whether biological, organizational, or cognitive — are inherently fragile because they have only one response mode.
- **Resilience requires variability.** Healthy variability indicates that a system's regulatory mechanisms are active and responsive. Low variability signals atrophied regulation — the system has optimized for a narrow set of conditions and will break when those conditions change.
- **Resilience requires slack.** Intentional margin — in time, resources, energy, space — provides room for the system to self-organize in response to perturbation. Over-optimization eliminates slack, making the system efficient under expected conditions but catastrophically fragile under unexpected ones.
- **Resilience is not the same as stability.** Stable systems resist change; resilient systems absorb it. A volatile system (many shallow attractors, high diversity) may be highly unstable yet highly resilient, because it can shift rapidly between states without breaking. A fragile system (few deep attractors, low diversity) may be very stable under normal conditions yet catastrophically non-resilient when pushed past its threshold.
- **Antifragility goes beyond resilience.** Nassim Taleb distinguishes three responses to perturbation: fragile systems break, resilient systems recover, antifragile systems get stronger. Antifragility is what hormesis, the edge-of-chaos principle, and adaptive variability describe — systems that use perturbation as fuel for improvement rather than merely surviving it.
