# Hebbian Learning

The fundamental neurological learning principle: neurons that activate together strengthen their connections ("fire together, wire together"), and unused pathways weaken and dissolve ("fire apart, wire apart"). This bidirectional plasticity means the brain is constantly reshaping itself based on what is practiced and what is neglected. Frequency and distribution of practice matter more than volume -- the brain consolidates between sessions, not during them.

## Key points

- **Co-activation creates and strengthens pathways.** When two neurons repeatedly fire at the same time, the synaptic connection between them strengthens. This is the mechanism behind all learned behaviors, memories, motor patterns, and habits -- repeated co-activation encodes them into the neural architecture. The more a pathway is used, the more efficient and automatic it becomes.
- **The reverse is equally true.** Pathways that are not activated weaken and eventually dissolve. Skills, memories, and movement capacities that are not practiced fade -- not because they are "forgotten" but because the neural substrate that supported them has been reallocated. Use it or lose it is not a metaphor but a description of synaptic pruning.
- **Less quantity + higher frequency = superior results.** The brain needs time, energy, and sleep between sessions to consolidate learning. Practicing briefly but frequently (distributed practice) produces stronger and more durable neural pathways than practicing intensely but infrequently (massed practice). This applies to motor skills, language learning, cognitive skills, and habit formation alike.
- **Sedentarism has a neurological dimension.** Repeating the same narrow set of movements (sitting, typing, scrolling) for hours daily creates deeply entrenched neural pathways for those patterns while allowing pathways for unused movements (squatting, hanging, crawling) to dissolve. The body does not merely become physically stiff -- the brain loses the wiring to coordinate those movements.
- **Two reinforcement mechanisms: repetition and emotion.** Repetition produces gradual, incremental strengthening of pathways (the slow route). Emotional charge -- particularly fear, pain, or survival-relevant experiences -- produces rapid, sometimes single-trial consolidation (the fast route). This is why traumatic events are learned instantly while skill development takes thousands of repetitions.

## Examples

- **Pavlov's dog (bell-salivation).** Repeated pairing of bell and food created a neural pathway linking an auditory stimulus to a digestive response. The neurons encoding "bell sound" and "salivation" fired together enough times that the bell alone could trigger salivation -- a new pathway that did not exist before conditioning.
- **One pull-up practiced 4-5 times daily vs. one exhausting session weekly.** Practicing a single pull-up every time you pass a doorway bar (distributed, low-volume, high-frequency) builds strength and neural coordination faster than one weekly session of maximum-effort sets. The brain consolidates the motor pattern between each brief exposure, while the weekly session produces fatigue that limits quality repetitions.
- **Foreign words repeated briefly throughout the day vs. 30 minutes of mass repetition.** Reviewing vocabulary cards for 2 minutes six times per day produces better retention than a single 30-minute session. Each brief exposure triggers reconsolidation of the memory trace, and the intervals between exposures allow the brain to strengthen the pathway during rest.
