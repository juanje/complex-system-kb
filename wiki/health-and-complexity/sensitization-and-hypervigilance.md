# Sensitization and Hypervigilance

When tissue is injured or chronically painful, the nervous system removes inhibitory filters and amplifies signals from that region -- identical stimuli from injured versus healthy tissue produce different perceived intensity. In acute injury, this hypervigilance is adaptive: it forces attention and protection. In chronic pain, the amplification persists after tissue healing, becoming self-sustaining. The neural "volume" stays high, the somatosensory map degrades, and any unusual signal from the region -- fatigue, tension, even normal sensation -- is interpreted as the familiar pain.

## Key points

- **The brain uses inhibition to manage sensory data.** The brain processes all incoming sensory information but uses inhibitory filters to surface only relevant signals to consciousness. Most sensory input never reaches awareness because it is actively suppressed.
- **Injury removes filters and raises signal volume.** When tissue is damaged, the nervous system removes inhibitory filters for that region and amplifies incoming signals. This makes the area hypersensitive -- lower thresholds for activation and higher perceived intensity for the same physical stimulus.
- **In chronic pain, hypervigilance outlasts healing.** The neural amplification that was adaptive during injury persists after tissue repair. The nervous system maintains the high-volume setting "just in case," creating a state where normal sensory input is perceived as threatening or painful.
- **Somatosensory map degradation creates a blurry zone.** Chronic pain degrades the cortical representation of the affected area. Where the brain once had a precise map, it now has a blurry region. This degradation has two consequences: false positive pain responses to normal stimuli (the brain cannot distinguish threatening from benign signals in the blurry zone), and reduced motor control (precise movement requires a precise map).
- **Fire department metaphor.** A well-mapped neighborhood (sharp somatosensory representation) gets a proportionate response to emergencies -- one firefighter with an extinguisher. A blurry neighborhood (degraded map) triggers disproportionate responses -- the entire department mobilizes because the system cannot accurately assess the situation.
- **Understanding pain mechanisms is itself therapeutic.** Pain neuroscience education -- teaching patients how sensitization, map degradation, and hypervigilance work -- demonstrably reduces pain intensity. Changing the cognitive framework changes the brain's threat assessment, which changes the pain output.
- **Habituation through controlled exposure reverses amplification.** Creating repeated non-painful experiences in the sensitized region progressively teaches the brain that the area is safe. Each pain-free exposure provides evidence that the volume can be lowered, gradually restoring normal filtering.
- **Fragility spiral is a positive feedback loop.** Pain → feeling fragile → everything seems more dangerous → increased vigilance → lower thresholds → more pain → less movement → worse maps → more pain. Each element reinforces the next, creating a self-sustaining cycle that is difficult to exit without deliberate intervention.

## Examples

- **"Everything hits my wound."** After an injury, a person notices that the injured area seems to get bumped, brushed, or stressed far more than before. This is not Murphy's Law but selective attention -- the removed inhibitory filters make every contact with the region consciously salient, creating the impression of increased frequency.
- **Chronic pain patients misjudging touch location.** Patients with chronic back pain asked to identify where they are being touched on their back consistently misjudge by centimeters. Their somatosensory map has degraded, and precise localization is no longer possible.
- **Drawing the affected body part disproportionately.** When chronic pain patients draw their body, they consistently represent the painful region as larger or more distorted than its actual proportions. The neural map's distortion is reflected in conscious body image.
- **Ankle pain protocol: towel to mountain.** A sensitized ankle is progressively desensitized through graded exposure: wrinkled towel textures under the foot → flat surface standing → uneven surface standing → walking on flat → walking on gravel → hiking on trails. Each stage provides the nervous system with evidence that reduces the threat level.
