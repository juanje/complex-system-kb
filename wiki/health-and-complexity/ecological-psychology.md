# Ecological Psychology

Ecological psychology studies the organism-environment system as an indivisible unit, rejecting the idea that cognition happens only inside the head. Rather than treating perception as internal mental processing of representations, this approach reframes perception and action as direct engagement with the environment. The organism does not build a model of the world and then decide how to act -- it perceives opportunities for action directly in the environment and responds in real time. This perspective connects naturally to complex systems thinking: both reject reductionism and emphasize relationships and context over isolated components.

## Key points

- **The organism-environment system is the unit of analysis.** You cannot meaningfully study an organism apart from its environment, or an environment apart from the organisms that inhabit it. The two form an indivisible system whose properties emerge from their interaction. Studying either in isolation strips away the very relationships that generate behavior.
- **Affordances are relational opportunities for action.** Affordances are not properties of objects or of agents alone -- they emerge from the relationship between the two. A staircase affords climbing for an able-bodied person but not for someone in a wheelchair. A chair affords sitting for a human but not for a fish. In health and movement, affordances determine what actions are available to a person, making them central to understanding rehabilitation, training, and behavior change. Because affordances emerge from interaction, they cannot be fully predicted by analyzing the agent or environment in isolation -- a hallmark of complexity.
- **Cognition is embodied and extended.** Thinking is not a purely brain-based process. Embodied cognition recognizes that having a body -- with its specific capabilities, limitations, and sensory systems -- shapes how we think. Extended cognition goes further: cognitive processes extend beyond the brain into tools, artifacts, and the environment itself. Writing, calculating with pen and paper, or navigating with a map are all examples of cognition distributed across brain, body, and environment. These are not metaphors -- they are descriptions of how adaptive agents actually operate in complex systems.
- **Direct perception replaces representational models.** Rather than perceiving raw data and constructing internal models, organisms perceive meaningful features of their environment directly -- edges to grasp, surfaces to walk on, gaps to jump across. This direct perception is what makes real-time adaptive action possible in dynamic, unpredictable environments.
- **Ecological psychology provides theoretical grounding for complexity in health and movement.** Understanding how agents perceive and act in context -- through affordances, direct perception, and embodied cognition -- provides the theoretical foundation for treating health as a complex problem rather than a mechanical one.
- **Perception-action is a continuous cycle, not two sequential steps.** We perceive to move and move to perceive; each action changes the relationship with the environment and generates new information guiding the next action. There is no starting point -- it is an unbroken loop.
- **Three layers of environment interact to shape affordances.** The physical layer (surfaces, objects), the internal/bodily layer (fatigue, pain, hormones, emotional state), and the sociocultural layer (norms, expectations, language, tools) all modulate what the environment "offers" for action.
- **Affordances are modulated by all three layers.** Fatigue (internal) changes what a flight of stairs "offers"; being told "stairs are dangerous for your back" (sociocultural) tints that affordance with threat. The same physical environment generates different affordances depending on the other two layers.

## Examples

- **Affordances in rehabilitation.** A recovering patient's movement affordances change as they heal -- what their body can do in relation to their environment shifts over time. Effective rehabilitation works with this changing affordance landscape rather than imposing fixed exercise protocols, adapting the environment and the challenges to the patient's evolving capabilities.
- **Embodied cognition in sports.** A basketball player does not calculate trajectories -- they perceive the affordance of a pass directly, shaped by years of calibrated perception-action coupling. Their cognition is inseparable from their body's capabilities and their history of interaction with the game environment.
- **Extended cognition in everyday life.** A notebook extends memory; a calculator extends arithmetic; a map extends spatial reasoning. In each case, the cognitive process is distributed across brain, body, and tool -- not contained within the skull. This is relevant to understanding how people navigate complex health decisions using external resources, social networks, and environmental cues.
- **Recommended reading.** The author points to Blau and Wagman's "Introduction to Ecological Psychology" and Rob Gray's "How We Learn to Move" as essential background for understanding health and movement through this lens.
- **Chronic back pain and stairs -- three layers, one behavior.** The physical environment has stairs, the internal environment (pain and fear of movement) makes those stairs feel threatening, and the sociocultural environment ("be careful with your back") reinforces avoidance. Same stairs, three layers of environment, one emergent behavior: avoidance.
