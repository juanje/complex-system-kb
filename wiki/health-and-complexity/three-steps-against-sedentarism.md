# Three Steps Against Sedentarism

A practical framework of three high-impact, universally accessible actions -- walk more, sit on the floor more, hang from your hands more -- selected via the Pareto principle to address the most common sedentary dysfunctions with the lowest barrier to entry. These three interventions target the highest-leverage points in the sedentary dysfunction cascade, designed to break inertia and create positive feedback loops that drive further change.

## Key points

- **Selected by the 80/20 rule.** Rather than prescribing a comprehensive exercise program, these three actions were chosen because they deliver the greatest impact on the most common sedentary problems with the least friction. They are the minimum effective intervention that initiates systemic change.
- **Walking addresses insufficient daily movement.** Walking is the baseline movement the human body expects. Its absence creates systemic deficiency across cardiovascular, musculoskeletal, cognitive, and metabolic systems. It is the "fat" in the movement-as-nutrition framework -- the macronutrient the body needs constantly.
- **Floor sitting addresses lower-body sedentary dysfunction.** Chair sitting specializes the body for a single posture, creating a cascade of problems: rigid ankles, unstable knees, shortened hip flexors, weakened glutes, unstable trunk. Floor sitting reverses this by demanding greater ranges of motion, engaging more muscles, and naturally prompting postural variety.
- **Hanging addresses upper-body sedentary dysfunction.** Modern life positions almost all activity in front of the body at or below shoulder height, atrophying overhead capacity and posterior chain function. Hanging provides the overhead traction stimulus the shoulder evolved to receive, addressing shoulder pain, neck tension, and grip weakness simultaneously.
- **All three share critical design features.** Universally accessible (no age, fitness, or equipment barrier), adaptable to all levels through progressive scaling, require no gym or special equipment, and integrate easily into existing daily routines rather than requiring dedicated "exercise time."
- **Designed as inertia-breaking intervention.** The framework is not a training program but a catalyst. By creating immediate, tangible improvement with minimal effort, it initiates a positive feedback loop: feeling better leads to wanting more movement, which leads to moving more, which leads to feeling even better.
- **The sedentary dysfunction cascade.** Sedentarism creates a chain of compensations: weak feet lead to rigid ankles, which produce unstable knees, which cause rigid or weak hips, which create an unstable trunk, which results in rigid shoulders and a tense neck. Walking, floor sitting, and hanging target the highest-leverage points in this chain.
- **Breathing as a fourth foundational step.** Breathing mechanics affect stress regulation, posture, trunk stability, and autonomic balance. While not one of the "three steps," it functions as a foundational capacity that amplifies the benefits of the other three.

## Examples

- **The positive feedback catalyst.** A common client result after implementing the three steps: "My body wants more. What should I do now?" The framework succeeds precisely because it was designed to create this demand -- the client's own system begins requesting more movement, replacing external motivation with internal drive.
