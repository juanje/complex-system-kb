# Movement as Nutrition

Framework by [Katy Bowman](../authors/katy-bowman.md) treating movement variety as nutritional diversity for cellular health. Movement literally feeds cells: muscle contraction drives blood and nutrients to body parts that have no other way to receive them. Cell-level adaptation is local, not systemic -- exercising one leg produces gene-expression changes only in that leg. This reframes sedentarism not as simple inactivity but as movement malnutrition.

## Key points

- **Movement feeds cells mechanically.** Muscle contraction is what drives blood, oxygen, and nutrients into tissues. Without movement, cells in specific regions are literally starved -- not metaphorically, but physiologically. This makes movement a delivery mechanism, not just an energy expenditure activity.
- **Adaptation is local, not systemic.** The body does not adapt globally to exercise -- it adapts at the cellular level, locally. Exercising one leg produces gene-expression changes only in that leg, not the other. This means variety in movement patterns matters as much as total volume, because different movements feed different cells.
- **The nutrition metaphor maps movement types to macronutrients.** Low-intensity, frequent movement like walking functions as dietary fats -- the baseline energy substrate the body needs constantly. High-intensity, infrequent movement maps to other macronutrients needed in smaller doses. Fine-motor, precise movements are the micronutrients -- small amounts with outsized functional importance.
- **Even athletes can be movement-malnourished.** A runner who logs high mileage but never crawls, hangs, squats, or moves laterally has a movement diet as narrow as someone who eats only chicken breast. High volume in one pattern does not compensate for absence of others, because the cells fed by those missing patterns remain undernourished.
- **This framework shifts the intervention target.** Instead of prescribing "more exercise," the question becomes: what movements are missing from your diet? This makes the problem tractable and personal rather than generic.

## Examples

- **The one-leg experiment.** Studies showing gene-expression changes in the exercised leg but not the resting leg of the same person -- demonstrating that adaptation is local and movement must reach specific tissues to affect them.
- **Walking as the "fat" of movement.** Just as dietary fat is the baseline macronutrient the body runs on, low-intensity walking throughout the day is the movement substrate that keeps all systems minimally fed -- and its absence creates systemic deficiency.
- **The athlete with movement deficiencies.** A marathon runner with chronic shoulder pain and poor grip strength -- high movement volume in one pattern, starvation in others. The nutrition lens reveals the gap that "hours of exercise per week" obscures.
