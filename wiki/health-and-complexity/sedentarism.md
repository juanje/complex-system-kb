# Sedentarism

Sedentarism is not simply sitting too much or moving too little — it is the over-specialization of movement patterns, reducing the variety of stimuli the body receives. It is primarily driven by environment and culture rather than individual choice. The modern environment — chairs, shoes, screens, flat floors — acts as an attractor pulling behavior toward narrow, repetitive patterns that the body then optimizes for at the expense of all other capacities.

## Key points

- Sedentarism is over-specialization of movement, not merely insufficient movement — the body optimizes for what it does most and discards capacity for everything else
- Almost every profession has characteristic injuries from repeated narrow patterns, making occupational overuse a form of sedentarism
- The body adapts ruthlessly to demand: astronauts lose up to 40% bone density in weeks once gravitational loading is removed, demonstrating how quickly unused capacity is shed
- Environment drives sedentarism more than individual choice — chairs enforce hip flexion, shoes eliminate foot articulation, flat floors remove balance challenges, screens fix gaze distance
- Even athletes can be functionally sedentary: most sports reinforce the same narrow movement planes (hip flexion-extension dominates running, cycling, and squats), making sport-specific overuse injuries a form of sedentarism applied to sport
- "Movement junk food" — high-frequency, low-variability movement that crowds out healthier, more varied patterns, analogous to calorie-dense but nutrient-poor food
- Being physically active 3 hours per week only makes someone roughly 4% more active than a fully sedentary person, highlighting how the remaining 165 hours dominate adaptation
- The modern environment functions as an attractor basin, channeling behavior toward sedentary defaults that require deliberate effort to escape

## Examples

- Professional injuries as sedentarism: office workers develop neck/wrist issues, dentists get back pain, musicians suffer repetitive strain — all from over-specialized movement
- Astronaut bone density: removing gravitational load causes rapid bone loss, proving the body discards what it does not use
- Sedentary athletes: a person exercising 3 hours/week is only ~4% more active than someone fully sedentary — the other 165 hours of the week still shape adaptation
- Hip movement poverty: running, cycling, squats, and sitting all use the hip in flexion-extension only, neglecting rotation, abduction, adduction, and circumduction
